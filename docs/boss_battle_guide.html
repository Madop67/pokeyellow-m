01 These aren't the fights you remember

Kanto's bosses have been rebuilt from the ground up. If your plan is "bring the one type that beats theirs," you're going to get surprised.

Full teams

Every Gym Leader now brings six Pokémon, not two or three. The early gyms still sit at low levels — but there are a lot more of them.

Two-type identity

Each boss carries a second theme woven through their roster and moves, so a single hard counter no longer sweeps the whole team.

Real movesets

Bosses run four competitive moves — STAB, coverage for your usual answers, and a setup or status move — instead of random level-up filler.

Terastallizing aces

The last Pokémon a boss sends out Terastallizes on entry, changing its type and powering up its attacks. Plan your endgame around it.

The short version: bosses are now designed teams that answer the obvious counter. Coverage moves punish your safe switch-ins, the whole squad shares a defensive theme, and the ace flips the board right at the end. Come with a balanced party and a way to deal chip damage, not a single silver bullet.

02 How the boss AI plays against you

Boss trainers make deliberate decisions. Knowing their habits is half the battle.

  1. They target the matchup, not at randomA boss reads how your active Pokémon lines up and picks the move that actually threatens it. Don't count on them wasting a turn on a resisted attack.
  2. They open with a signature stat boostOn a fresh Pokémon's first turn, most bosses reach for their trademark X-item — X Special, X Defend, X Speed, and so on (see the table below). If you let them set up for free, the next hit lands a lot harder.
  3. They cure status — but never heal HPA boss will use a Full Heal to shake off sleep or freeze, so those aren't a permanent lock. What they won't do is restore HP: there's no Full Restore stalling. Chip damage sticks, so racing them down is a real strategy.
  4. They'll switch out of a bad matchupIf your Pokémon threatens theirs super-effectively and they have a safer answer on the bench, they'll pivot to it — once per Pokémon. Leading with a hard counter may just chase them into their check, so bait the switch before you commit.
  5. The ace Terastallizes on send-outThe final Pokémon changes type the instant it appears. A weakness you were counting on may vanish; a new super-effective angle may open up. Save the right answer for it.

03 Ace Terastallization — the twist at the end

Every boss's last Pokémon becomes a pure Tera-Type the moment it's sent out, for the rest of the battle.

Terastallizing does two things at once: it changes the ace's defensive typing (its old weaknesses may disappear) and it supercharges attacks of the Tera-Type — moves matching it hit for 2× STAB when the type was already one of the Pokémon's own. Most aces Tera into a type they already had, turning their signature attack into a wrecking ball. A few use it to shed a crippling weakness — watch those closely.

07 How to prepare

A few habits that carry you through every boss on this list.

Balance > counter

One hard counter gets switched around or Tera'd out of. Build a party that covers several angles.

Race, don't stall

Bosses never heal HP. Consistent chip damage always adds up — a stall war is one you can win.

Answer the ace last

Keep the Pokémon that beats the Tera'd ace in reserve; don't spend it clearing the early team.

Pack status insurance

Sleep, confusion, and crits show up on nearly every team. Full Heals and a bit of bulk smooth the variance.
