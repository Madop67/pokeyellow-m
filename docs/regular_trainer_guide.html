Pokémon Chromatic Yellow · Trainer's Field Guide

Route Trainers: No More Free Wins The bosses were only half the story. Every ordinary trainer in Kanto — bug catchers, hikers, swimmers, the lot — now fields a real four-move set and picks their attacks with actual battle sense. The one-move Magikarp is dead. Here's what changed, and what to watch for from Viridian Forest to Victory Road.

Covers: ~300 regular-trainer battles · 24 trainer classes Companion to: boss_battle_guide.html

01 The filler is gone

In the original game, the trainer between you and the next town was mostly a speed bump — a Pokémon with one move, or two, or four moves that made no sense together. Not anymore.

Four real moves

Every Pokémon whose learnset can support it now carries four deliberate moves — a STAB attack, coverage, and often a stat or status trick — drawn from what that species can actually learn.

Smarter targeting

Even the lowliest bug catcher now reads the type matchup and reaches for the move that hurts you, instead of firing at random.

Level-appropriate

The power is capped by level, so early routes stay early. A Route 3 mon won't suddenly hit you with a 120-power nuke — that's saved for the endgame.

Still below the bosses

Regular trainers keep their original teams, levels, and no items. They're sharper, not scaled up — a clear tier under the Gym Leaders.

The short version: you can no longer autopilot through routes by spamming your strongest attack. A team of "weak" trainers can now chip you down between Pokémon Centers, land a paralysis you didn't expect, or set up a sweep if you ignore them. Heal more often, and stop underestimating the small fights.

02 How the new trainer AI thinks

The upgrade isn't just better movesets — it's better decisions. Every trainer now runs at least a baseline "battle brain," and some classes run a lot more than that.

  1. They don't waste a status move that can't workA trainer won't try to poison your Poison- or Steel-type, paralyze your Electric-type, put a Ground-type to sleep with an Electric move, or Leech Seed your Grass-type. If a status can't land, they reach for something that can.
  2. They lean into super-effective hitsEvery trainer now weighs the type matchup: they favor moves that are super-effective against your active Pokémon (and favor them more against a double weakness), and they steer away from resisted attacks. Moves you're outright immune to are dropped entirely.
  3. Some classes love to set upA handful of "brainy" classes — Poké Maniacs, Super Nerds, Psychics, Scientists, Gentlemen — are nudged toward stat-boosts and tricky status moves. Expect a Meditate, an Agility, a Sand-Attack before the real damage starts.
  4. A few will actually act on the battlefieldJugglers switch Pokémon to dodge a bad matchup. Black Belts pop an X Attack to hit harder. These aren't bosses, but they'll spend a turn to gain an edge — don't assume a route trainer will just stand there and trade.
  5. Cool Trainers fight like mini-bossesThe Cool Trainers guarding Victory Road get the full boss AI package: best-damage move selection, an opening X-item, status cures, and a switch out of bad matchups. Treat them with the same respect you'd give a Gym junior — because mechanically, that's what they are.

04 Adjusting your habits

Nothing here is unfair — but the old reflexes will get you chipped down. A few adjustments carry you through Kanto comfortably.

  1. Heal between fights, not between townsRoute trainers now do real cumulative damage. Topping off at the Center on the way out isn't enough for a long route — carry Potions and use them.
  2. Respect the setup classesAgainst Poké Maniacs, Super Nerds, Psychics, and Black Belts, don't give a free turn. If they're about to boost or paralyze, hit first or switch to something that doesn't care.
  3. Don't over-commit your hard counterJugglers and Cool Trainers switch. Leading with your one perfect answer can just chase them into their check — sometimes it's better to bait the pivot, then bring it in.
  4. Bring coverage, not a single sweeperBecause every trainer now reads the matchup and answers your type, a one-Pokémon run gets worn down. A balanced party with two or three offensive types is the smooth path.
  5. Mind your PP on the long stretchesRock Tunnel, the sea routes, Victory Road — no Centers, lots of trainers, all now demanding real attacks. Ethers and a second attacking move matter more than they used to.
